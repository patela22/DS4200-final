Analyzing Northeastern Rate My Professor Data

By: Paul Champagne, Trung Nguyen, Ansh Patel, & Owen Sharpe

Topics, Tasks, and Goals

Finding quality professors is essential for a positive college experience, particularly at large institutions like Northeastern University. Our research objective aims to identify which colleges and departments within Northeastern have the best-rated professors, utilizing data from Rate My Professor. Specifically, our research question focuses on understanding the distribution of average professor ratings across various departments. By analyzing subjective rating data, we intend to provide valuable insights that can assist students in making informed decisions when selecting courses. Additionally, this analysis will offer the university a comprehensive view of teaching quality across its colleges, pinpointing areas of strength and opportunities for improvement.

Data

We began by collecting essential information about professors, specifically their names and IDs. Utilizing an available API, we retrieved this foundational data, serving as a gateway to enhancing our dataset. With the IDs, we then proceeded to scrape additional information, gathering a comprehensive array of details such as department affiliations, college names, ratings, levels of difficulty, and reviews. Once we amassed this dataset, which consisted of 3,494 rows and 15 columns, we undertook an essential preprocessing phase. This step involved cleaning the data to ensure its quality and reliability. We focused on identifying and addressing NaN values, duplicates, and any inconsistencies that could compromise our analysis. This careful setup laid the groundwork for creating visual representations that could help explain the complexities of professor ratings across various departments and colleges.

Distribution of Average Rating of Professors among different colleges

The histogram above shows the distribution of average ratings for professors in different Northeastern Colleges. These colleges have a solid incremental up trend in their ratings, where as the ratings go up, so does the number of professors. COE and DMSB also have a generally more skewed right distribution, but it is not as evident the previously mentioned colleges. Khoury has the most mixed distribution compared to all colleges at Northeastern, with their ratings distributed in the 2.0 to 5.0 range. Overall, the main takaway from these histograms is that colleges who are more skewed right have a general tendency to have more professors that are liked by its students and that Northeastern generally has professors that have high ratings of 4 or above and the highests frequency for all colleges are professors with a rating of 4.5 to 5. This can help guide students when debating in which college(s) they want to study in, given that higher ratings are favoured.

Scatterplot Plot Relation with Average Professor Rating and Course Difficulty

Students across universities may be under the belief that the more difficult a course is, the more likely that professor will have a lower rating. However, from the scatterplots above, which depicts the relationship between Average Rating and Level of Difficulty for professors across Northeastern colleges, we can see the correlations are all somewhat negative, but not to the extent one may expect. For most colleges we see fairly mild negative correlations, ranging from -0.37 to -0.52, which cannot concretely denote that higher levels of difficulties correlate with lower average ratings. The only expectations are for Bouve and Khoury, which respectively have -0.67 and -0.20 correlations. Overall, these scatterplots show that we can fairly confident that levels of difficulty do not indicate lower average ratings as the plot depict a negligible correlation and an even poorer R^2, showing that the linear regression model does not encapsulate the data well. This highlights that for a majority of colleges at NEU, students should not necessarily be biased towards certain professors simply because of the difficulty of the course and that a higher rating does not necessarily signify that a professor is teaching an easier course.

Sentiment Score vs Average Rating for Professors from Northeastern Colleges (5+ Ratings)

Our college professor scatter plot displays the relationship between sentiment scores and average ratings for professors across different colleges at Northeastern University. Each point represents a professor with at least 5 ratings, color-coded by their respective college. There is a strong positive correlation between sentiment scores and average ratings. This trend suggests that professors with a more positive sentiment in their reviews generally receive higher average ratings. Most ratings range between 2.0 and 4.5, while sentiment scores range from around -0.10 to 0.40. A few outliers with lower ratings (~1.5) and sentiment scores (< 0.00) suggest some professors receive consistently negative feedback. Professors from all colleges are spread across the entire range, indicating that no single college lies in a specific sentiment score or rating range. Bouve College of Health Sciences seemed to perform as the best college, clustering in the higher sentiment and rating ranges.

Boxplot of Average Ratings for Professors from Different Northeastern colleges

The boxplots above highlight the distribution of average ratings for professors at Northeastern's colleges. As we can see, a vast majority of colleges seem to have approximately equal distributions, this is most evident in the first five boxplots (from DMSB to CAMD), where the IQR lies in between 3.0 and 4.5, and the median lies at approximately 3.7. However, we do so see some slight differences for the last 3 colleges. Khoury and the COS have more spread out distributions (as shown by their wider IQRs) that are centered at lower medians (approximately 3.4 for Khoury and 3.5 for COS). On the other hand, CPS has a more concentrated distribution, highlighted by its smaller IQR and shorter tails, with a median that is higher than the rest of Northeastern colleges, although only by a small margin, laying right below the 4.0 level. Overall, the main takeaway from these histograms is that average ratings for Northeastern professors lay on the upper half of the 1-5 average rating scale on Rate My Professor. Of course, these distributions do not exemplify a "ranking" of Northeastern colleges but, it most definitely can help guide students in seeing how professor ratings are distributed when aggregated, which can have an influence on what they decide to study.

Conclusion

Our analysis of Northeastern University's Rate My Professor data offers insights into the distribution of professor ratings across various colleges and departments. A primary finding is the generally positive distribution of professor ratings across Northeastern, with most colleges having a significant proportion of professors rated between 4.0 and 5.0. Colleges such as Bouve College of Health Sciences, the College of Social Sciences and Humanities, and the College of Science exhibited right-skewed distributions, indicating a higher frequency of well-rated professors. Conversely, Khoury College of Computer Sciences displayed a more mixed distribution, suggesting higher variability in student experiences. Our sentiment analysis further reinforced this trend, showing a strong positive correlation between sentiment scores and average ratings. Professors who receive more favorable reviews tend to have higher overall ratings, with Bouve College standing out for its consistently high sentiment and rating scores. On the other hand, Khoury and the School of Law had lower average ratings and sentiment scores, suggesting areas where student satisfaction may be lower or more variable. Interestingly, the relationship between a professor's rating and the perceived difficulty of their course was not as straightforward as one might assume. While there were mild negative correlations across most colleges, they were not strong enough to conclusively link higher difficulty with lower ratings. This information can benefit students seeking specific learning environments or teaching methods. Overall, our research provides a comprehensive overview of teaching quality across Northeastern University. Most colleges feature highly rated professors, and while course difficulty plays a role in student evaluations, it is not the sole determinant of a professor's rating. These insights can help students choose professors and courses that align with their academic goals and learning preferences while offering the university a data-driven perspective on areas of strength and opportunities for improvement.
