Analyzing Northeastern Rate My Professor Data

By: Paul Champagne, Trung Nguyen, Ansh Patel, & Owen Sharpe

Topics, Tasks, and Goals

Finding quality professors is essential for a positive college experience, particularly at large institutions like Northeastern University. Our research objective aims to identify which colleges and departments within Northeastern have the best-rated professors, utilizing data from Rate My Professor. Specifically, our research question focuses on understanding the distribution of average professor ratings across various departments. By analyzing subjective rating data, we intend to provide valuable insights that can assist students in making informed decisions when selecting courses. Additionally, this analysis will offer the university a comprehensive view of teaching quality across its colleges, pinpointing areas of strength and opportunities for improvement.

Data

We began by collecting essential information about professors, specifically their names and IDs. Utilizing an available API, we retrieved this foundational data, serving as a gateway to enhancing our dataset. With the IDs, we then proceeded to scrape additional information, gathering a comprehensive array of details such as department affiliations, college names, ratings, levels of difficulty, and reviews. Once we amassed this dataset, which consisted of 3,494 rows and 15 columns, we undertook an essential preprocessing phase. This step involved cleaning the data to ensure its quality and reliability. We focused on identifying and addressing NaN values, duplicates, and any inconsistencies that could compromise our analysis. This careful setup laid the groundwork for creating visual representations that could help explain the complexities of professor ratings across various departments and colleges.

Distribution of Average Rating of Professors among different colleges

The histogram above shows the distribution of average ratings for professors in different Northeastern Colleges. The user can interact with the chart by choosing the college of their choice with the drop down selector, and the histograms will update to reflect the user input. We can see a common pattern in the distribution of ratings for CSSH, Bouve College, CAMD, COS, and CPS. These colleges have a solid incremental up trend in their ratings, where as the ratings go up, so does the number of professors. COE and DMSB also have a generally more skewed right distribution, but it is not as evident the previously mentioned colleges. Khoury has the most mixed distribution compared to all colleges at Northeastern, with their ratings distributed in the 2.0 to 5.0 range. Overall, the main takaway from these histograms is that colleges who are more skewed right have a general tendency to have more professors that are liked by its students and that Northeastern generally has professors that have high ratings of 4 or above and the highests frequency for all colleges are professors with a rating of 4.5 to 5. This can help guide students when debating in which college(s) they want to study in, given that higher ratings are favoured.

Heatmap of Metric Distribution by Different Majors

The dataset explores ratings and metrics across various colleges and departments at Northeastern University, focusing on attributes like average ratings, number of ratings, and difficulty levels. It is structured as an interactive heatmap, where rows represent colleges and columns represent departments, with values encoded in color. The data includes hundreds of entries, sourced from academic evaluations or surveys, and allows users to filter by specific colleges or metrics for a customized view. This comprehensive structure is designed to offer insights into educational quality and performance trends across departments.

Students across universities may be under the belief that the more difficult a course is, the more likely that professor will have a lower rating. However, from the scatterplots above, which depicts the relationship between Average Rating and Level of Difficulty for professors across Northeastern colleges, we can see the correlations are all somewhat negative, but not to the extent one may expect. For most colleges we see fairly mild negative correlations, ranging from -0.37 to -0.52, which cannot concretely denote that higher levels of difficulties correlate with lower average ratings. The only expections are for Bouve and Khoury, which respectively have a -0.67 and -0.20 correlation. From these, we can be more confident that levels of difficulty do lead to lower average ratings for the former, while there is a negligible correlation for the latter. Overall, this goes to show that for a majority of colleges at NEU, students should not necessarily be biased towards certain professors simply because of the difficulty of the course and that, one the hand, a higher rating does not necessarily signify that a professor is teaching an easier course.

Sentiment Score vs Average Rating for Professors from Northeastern Colleges (20+ Ratings)

Our college professor scatter plot displays the relationship between sentiment scores and average ratings for professors across different colleges at Northeastern University. Each point represents a professor with at least 20 ratings, color-coded by their respective college. There is a strong positive correlation between sentiment scores and average ratings. This trend suggests that professors with a more positive sentiment in their reviews generally receive higher average ratings. Most ratings range between 2.0 and 4.5, while sentiment scores range from around -0.10 to 0.40. A few outliers with lower ratings (~1.5) and sentiment scores (< 0.00) suggest some professors receive consistently negative feedback. Professors from all colleges are spread across the entire range, indicating that no single college lies in a specific sentiment score or rating range. Bouve College of Health Sciences seemed to perform as the best college, clustering in the higher sentiment and rating ranges.

Conclusion

Our college averages scatter plot summarizes the average sentiment score and average rating for each college at Northeastern University. Similar to the first graph, there is a positive trend between the average sentiment score and average rating. Bouve College of Health Sciences has one of the highest average ratings (~3.85) and a relatively high sentiment score (~0.18), indicating a positive approval by many students. College of Science and D'Amore-McKim School of Business have moderate ratings and sentiment scores, clustered around 3.4 to 3.6 ratings and ~0.15 sentiment score. Khoury College of Computer Sciences and School of Law have the lowest average ratings and sentiment scores among the colleges displayed. An Unknown category has been included, with a relatively high rating and sentiment score. This category might have represented professors not formally associated with a single college or courses offered across multiple disciplines.
